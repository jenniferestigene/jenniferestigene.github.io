U.S. Gas Prices Through Major Economic Events

Tracking 30+ years of gasoline and diesel prices through recessions, conflicts, and global crises

Date July 2025 Tools Python, Matplotlib, Pandas Source U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) Type Time Series, Event Analysis

The Story

Gas prices serve as a powerful economic indicator, reflecting geopolitical tensions, natural disasters, financial crises, and shifts in global oil markets. This visualization traces over three decades of price fluctuations, annotating key moments that sent shockwaves through the American economy.

Major Events

The 2022 Russia-Ukraine War triggered the sharpest price spike in modern history, with diesel reaching an unprecedented $5.81 per gallon, 40% higher than during the 2008 financial crisis.

Key Findings

Several patterns emerge from the 30-year dataset:

  • War premium: Conflicts consistently drive immediate price spikes, with the Ukraine war causing the largest increase
  • Diesel volatility: Commercial fuel prices show higher volatility than consumer gasoline, reflecting supply chain sensitivity
  • COVID anomaly: The pandemic created the steepest price drop in history, followed by the fastest recovery
  • Recession buffer: Economic downturns typically correlate with price decreases due to reduced demand
  • Recovery lag: Prices tend to rise faster than they fall, creating asymmetric consumer impact

Design Decisions

The visualization employs several techniques to enhance readability and impact:

Recession shading: Gray vertical bands mark NBER-defined recession periods, providing economic context for price movements. This immediately shows how demand destruction during downturns affects fuel prices.

Event annotations: Five critical moments are highlighted with red markers and labels, creating a narrative arc through recent history. The placement balances information density with visual clarity.

Dual fuel types: Overlaying gasoline and diesel prices reveals the premium gap and how it fluctuates with market conditions, commercial diesel often leads consumer price movements.

Current price callouts: End-of-series labels provide immediate context for today's prices relative to historical trends.

Technical Details

  • Data Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA)
  • Frequency: Weekly price observations (Monday data releases)
  • Coverage: U.S. national average retail prices
  • Fuel Types: Regular gasoline (all formulations), Diesel (ULSD)
  • Methodology: Volume-weighted averages from 1,000 retail outlets
  • Tools: Python (pandas, matplotlib) for data processing and visualization
  • Event Dates: Verified against historical records
