Research Question

How effectively do governments protect fundamental human rights in climate disaster-prone regions, and how can data science models assess and improve state preparedness?

The Challenge

Climate disasters increasingly threaten fundamental human rights worldwide. Yet we lack systematic frameworks to evaluate whether government preparedness translates into actual protection of rights like shelter, health, water, food, and life.

The Approach

This thesis develops a data-driven preparedness classification model analyzing disaster events from 2000–2023, mapping disaster types to human rights impacts, and evaluating state responses globally.

Key Findings

Preparedness Varies Dramatically

Over half (52.1%) of disasters were classified as "Prepared," but 15.8% were "Not Prepared"—with stark regional disparities between Global North and South.

Disaster Type Gaps

Floods and earthquakes were well-managed, while droughts and extreme temperatures showed highest under-preparedness—revealing institutional gaps for slow-onset hazards.

Regional Vulnerabilities

Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia had highest frequencies of "Not Prepared" events. Europe and Oceania demonstrated stronger preparedness overall.

Widespread Rights Violations

88% of disaster events were associated with high-risk rights impacts, most frequently threatening health, shelter, life, water, and access to aid.

Preparedness ≠ Protection

Even high-preparedness countries (USA, China, India) experienced substantial rights violations—preparedness alone doesn't ensure rights protection.

Governance is Critical

Countries with lower governance scores were consistently less prepared and more rights-vulnerable. Governance effectiveness moderated all outcomes.

Methodology

A multi-stage analytical framework combining preparedness classification, human rights mapping, and advanced statistical techniques.

1

Classification Model

Multi-tier scoring using log-transformed thresholds for deaths, injuries, homelessness. Binary signals of state stress including international appeals and emergency declarations.

2

Rights Mapping

Custom framework mapping disaster subtypes to 8 fundamental rights: shelter, health, water, food, education, life, aid, and healthy environment.

3

Clustering Analysis

KMeans clustering grouped countries into preparedness profiles based on normalized disaster-type vectors, revealing distinct preparedness patterns.

4

Regression Models

OLS regression explored relationships between preparedness scores and rights impacts, controlling for disaster frequency and severity.

5

Geospatial Analysis

Choropleth maps visualized global preparedness index and rights violations per country, revealing geographic patterns.

6

Time Series

Year-by-year analysis examined preparedness trends and normalized distribution of preparedness levels over two decades.

Impact & Applications

A scalable framework for global monitoring and policy reform

Policy Applications

  • Global monitoring systems for disaster preparedness
  • Targeted policy reform for specific disaster types and regions
  • Resource allocation for climate adaptation
  • International development priorities

Key Insight

Traditional preparedness metrics must explicitly incorporate human rights protection as a core outcome measure—not a secondary consideration.
